Tech 
 
Felix Biestek's 7 Principles of Case Work - Digital Implementation 
Framework 
 
 
Principle 1: Individualization 
Clinical Definition: Recognizing each client as a unique individual with their own 
experiences, traumas, and needs. No two cases are identical. 
The Challenge in Gaza: In shelters with thousands of displaced people, workers 
naturally see a "crowd" rather than individuals. Aid fatigue leads to standardized 
responses. 
Tech Solution: Digital Case File System with QR Code identification. 
How It Works: 
- Every child receives a wristband with a unique QR code 
- The mobile app stores each child's specific trauma history, family status, and 
behavioral patterns 
- When a volunteer scans the QR code, the app displays custom guidance 
 
Example: 
- Volunteer scans child's QR code 
- App displays: "This child is an orphan. Both parents deceased. Avoid asking 
about 'your family.' Focus on 'your safety.'" 
- Outcome: The volunteer treats the child as an individual, not one of thousands 
 
 
Principle 2: Purposeful Expression of Feelings 
Clinical Definition: Allowing clients to express their emotions freely, especially 
negative feelings like anger, grief, and fear. Expression is the first step toward 
healing. 
The Challenge in Gaza: No private spaces exist in bombed-out shelters. Children 
cannot speak openly because others are listening. 
Tech Solution: "Green Zone" Geofencing + Digital Expression Tools 
How It Works: 
- The mesh network designates specific physical locations (tents, quiet corners) as 
"Green Zones" 
- When a volunteer enters a Green Zone with a child, the app triggers a calming 
interface 
- The app provides non-verbal expression tools: emoji grids, drawing canvas, digital 
sandtray 
 
 

Example: 
- Volunteer takes child to Green Zone tent 
- Volunteer opens Drawing Tool on tablet 
- Volunteer says: "You can show me how you feel. There is no wrong way." 
- Child draws the explosion they witnessed 
- Outcome:The child expresses grief without being forced to find words 
 
 
Principle 3: Controlled Emotional Involvement 
Clinical Definition: The worker responds with sensitivity and empathy but maintains 
professional objectivity. The worker feels with the client, not for the client. 
The Challenge in Gaza: Local volunteers are also traumatized. They may become 
overwhelmed by the child's pain, losing the ability to help. 
Tech Solution: Remote Therapist Relay + Emotional Check-In Prompts 
How It Works: 
- The app automatically prompts volunteers to log their own emotional state 
- When a case becomes too heavy, the volunteer can escalate to a remote therapist 
in Pakistan 
- The remote therapist guides the intervention while the volunteer acts as "hands and 
eyes" 
 
Example: 
- Volunteer feels overwhelmed by a child's story of losing their mother 
- Volunteer presses "Escalate" button in app 
- Remote therapist in Pakistan texts: "You have done well. Now step back. Let the child 
draw. I will guide you." 
- Outcome: Volunteer remains effective because emotional burden is shared with the 
remote therapist 
 
 
Principle 4: Acceptance 
Clinical Definition: Accepting the client as they are, including their weaknesses, 
fears, anger, and regressive behaviors. Acceptance is not approval; it is recognition 
of reality. 
The Challenge in Gaza: Children may exhibit regressive behaviors (bedwetting, 
thumb-sucking, baby talk) that frustrate exhausted volunteers. 
Tech Solution: Behavioral Normalization Prompts 
How It Works: 
- The app provides real-time scripts that normalize regressive behaviors 
- Volunteers are taught that regression is a sign of trauma, not "bad behavior" 
 
Example: 
- Child, age 8, starts wetting the bed and sucking thumb 
- Volunteer feels frustrated 

- App displays: "This child is regressing to an earlier developmental stage. This is normal 
for trauma. Do not shame. Say: 'Many children here are having accidents. It is okay. Let me 
help you clean up.'" 
- Outcome: The child is accepted as they are, not punished for trauma symptoms 
 
 
 
Principle 5: Non-Judgmental Attitude 
Clinical Definition: Avoiding judgment of the client's behavior, values, or coping 
mechanisms. The worker does not label behavior as "good" or "bad." 
The Challenge in Gaza: Traumatized children may act aggressively (biting, 
screaming, throwing things). Volunteers may naturally react with judgment or 
punishment. 
Tech Solution: "Crisis Mode" Script Shield 
How It Works: 
- The app features a "Crisis Mode" button for aggressive or dysregulated behavior 
- The AI generates a non-judgmental script for the volunteer to read 
 
Example: 
- Child throws food tray and screams "I hate you!" 
- Volunteer presses Crisis Mode button 
- App displays:"Do NOT say 'Stop being bad.' Say: 'I see you are very angry. That is okay. 
Can we sit together for one minute? No talking.'" 
- Volunteer reads script aloud calmly 
- Outcome: The child's aggression is accepted as a symptom of trauma, not punished as 
misbehavior 
 
 
Principle 6: Client Self-Determination 
Clinical Definition: Recognizing the client's right to make their own choices about 
their life and treatment. The worker guides but does not control. 
The Challenge in Gaza: In crisis situations, workers often take complete control 
because "we know what's best." This disempowers the child. 
Tech Solution: Choice Interface (A/B Options) 
How It Works: 
- The app presents two acceptable options to the child 
- The child chooses which one to do 
- The volunteer honors the choice 
 
Example: 
- Volunteer opens Choice Interface on tablet 
- App displays: "Ask the child: 'Do you want to draw a picture OR play with the sand tray?'" 
- Volunteer asks the question 
- Child points to sand tray 
- Volunteer says: "Good choice. Let's play with the sand together." 

- Outcome: The child exercises agency and control in a situation where everything else is 
out of their control 
 
 
Principle 7: Confidentiality 
Clinical Definition: Protecting the client's private information. The child's trauma 
history, family situation, and expressions are shared only with authorized 
professionals. 
The Challenge in Gaza: Multiple volunteers may work with the same child. 
Information can easily leak through gossip or careless conversation. 
Tech Solution: Encrypted Digital Case Files + Access Controls 
How It Works: 
- All case files are encrypted with AES-256 on the device 
- Files are transmitted only via encrypted mesh channels 
- Access is logged and audited 
- Volunteers see only what they need to see 
 
Example: 
- Volunteer A accesses a child's case file 
- App logs: Volunteer A, Device ID XYZ, Timestamp, Action: View 
- Volunteer B asks Volunteer A: "What happened to that child?" 
- Volunteer A's app displays reminder: "Do not discuss case details verbally. Refer 
Volunteer B to the system." 
- Outcome: The child's trauma story is protected from gossip and re-exposure
